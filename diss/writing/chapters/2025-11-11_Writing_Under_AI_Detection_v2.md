# Proof of Life: Writing Under AI Detection

_By Zach Muhlbauer_

As AI detection tools enter college classrooms, students are learning to write under algorithmic scrutiny. Across CUNY Reddit forums, they describe the quiet dread of submitting an essay that might be flagged as machine-written, even when every word is their own. What we often call “academic integrity” is being redefined through software that promises certainty but delivers anxiety, false positives, and distrust.

Writers like Marc Watkins and John Warner have shown how educational technologies often collapse the space for human judgment. Watkins warns that “the more we outsource evaluation to machines, the less room there is for empathy,” while Warner calls plagiarism detection a “shortcut around pedagogy” that replaces dialogue with surveillance. Their critique captures what CUNY students describe daily: the feeling that their labor is being measured by probability rather than presence.

When students write about AI detection online, they are narrating the lived realities of authorship under surveillance. Their posts reflect attempts to make sense of shifting expectations, to seek advice, and to translate personal frustration into shared understanding. What appears at first glance as complaint or venting often doubles as analysis: students tracing the fault lines where technology, assessment, and institutional authority meet. These posts reveal multilingual writers penalized for using Grammarly, first-generation students accused of cheating because their syntax seems “too clean,” and others so afraid of being flagged that they intentionally insert errors to appear human. This discourse shows how algorithmic oversight heightens existing inequities in language and access, recasting writing as a performance of authenticity.

Detection tools claim to preserve originality, yet they turn the classroom into a space where humanness must be proven. The same large language models that generate machine-written text also supply the training data for the detectors that claim to identify it. Neither can reliably tell the difference, yet both depend on that illusion for legitimacy. Leif Weatherby describes this dynamic as _remainder humanism_—a theoretical impasse in which we define the human only in contrast to the machine, clinging to an identity that automation continuously unsettles. Detection software enacts that impasse materially, treating writing as evidence and authorship as verification.

The technical unevenness of detection systems compounds this instability. Some rely on stylometric analysis, others on entropy metrics or classifiers trained on model-generated text. A few combine these methods with metadata harvesting. The result is an unstable ecology of evaluative technologies that promise objectivity while functioning as opaque infrastructures. Students experience this unevenness as uncertainty; they cannot anticipate how their prose will be read, only that it will be judged by systems unable to account for context, tone, or intention.

Much of this software also functions as a data grab, collecting and monetizing student writing under the banner of academic integrity. Essays become raw material for corporate datasets that train the next generation of detectors and generators alike. Sean Michael Morris and Jesse Stommel, in _A Guide for Resisting EdTech_, argue that such tools convert learning into an extractive process, turning intellectual labor into proprietary capital. Institutions deploy these systems in the name of fairness or consistency, yet in doing so they participate in a larger economy of surveillance and privatization that erodes pedagogical trust.

These dynamics intersect with what Karen Hao and John Borthwick call “the everything machine,” their term for the class of generative AI systems—ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini—that promise to do everything while redefining what counts as knowledge or authorship. Each new system absorbs the disruption it creates, presenting itself as both cause and cure. Derrida might call this structure a _pharmakon_—a substance that acts as both poison and remedy. Automation becomes the tool we reach for to treat the wounds automation has made. In higher education, this circular logic recasts teaching as crisis management, where new technologies arrive as solutions to the very displacements they produce.

Many instructors adopt detection software to safeguard the humanistic values they feel are under threat. They hope to preserve the sanctity of student authorship. Yet in doing so, they risk hollowing out the pedagogical spaces where those values might actually be practiced. When students must perform imperfection to appear legitimate, reflection gives way to self-surveillance. The act of writing becomes a form of risk management rather than inquiry or expression.

On Reddit, however, another kind of writing culture persists. Students post in the open about the challenges of coursework, the panic of detection accusations, and the subtle tactics they use to navigate them. They share detector results, compose appeal templates, and warn one another about unreliable tools. Writing in these forums becomes social rather than solitary, a process of negotiation and mutual aid. Through these exchanges, students create what might be called a vernacular pedagogy—an ethics of learning grounded in reciprocity and collective experimentation rather than compliance.

For educators, the challenge is to meet students in that space of negotiation. As Watkins and Warner remind us, the goal is not to ban technology but to restore conversation about what learning requires. AI detection is never only a technical system; it is a social condition that reveals how institutions define and value student labor. Addressing its harms means rebuilding trust, foregrounding collaboration, and treating writing as a relational act rather than a verification process. The work of teaching begins where automation ends, when we ask what it means to write, read, and learn together again.
