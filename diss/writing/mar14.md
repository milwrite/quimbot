## Literacy Crisis Reflection

Whether in discourse or in practice, there is a case to be made that we are living through a literacy crisis. Our present moment calls to mind controversial outcries from the past like *Johnny Can’t Write*. At the same time, colleges and universities do what they do best in making reactionary decisions to adopt new-fangled AI detection services to "solve" the problem. Yet, as literacy scholars and edupunks both remind us, these are not problems of technology that only technology can solve; they are always already social problems with wider political and economic implications than can be named in temrs of any one individual literate person. These efforts seek to enforce writing practices that no longer seem viable within the current economy of value through which students — especially working-class and first-generation college learners — read, write, and compose themselves across higher education. As John Trimbur argues, literacy crisis discourse is “always strategic,” condensing broader social and economic tensions into a manageable institutional problem while reasserting “the meritocratic educational order” (286). Writing into the present panic over AI-generated prose and Turnitin-style detection software, Gavin P. Johnson contends that the rise of “new digital media, new information economies, new means of knowledge production, and new technologies of surveillance” has extended this old crisis script into the present (169). The irony of literacy crisis discourse, then, is that it is Janus-faced. It casts students as the problem even as it registers a broader institutional crisis, namely a waning faith in the capacity of public-serving colleges and universities to sustain their mandate of mass education amid decades of disinvestment, adjunctification, tuition pressure, and the outsourcing of software platforms as one-size-fits-all solutions to the problem of college reading and writing. It also raises a more basic question, one that these institutional responses too often evade: where, exactly, are students reading and writing in college now?
