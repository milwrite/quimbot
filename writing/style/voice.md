# Voice — Tone, Register, and Context

*Load this for: matching register to audience, calibrating tone, distinguishing project contexts*

---

## Core Voice Profile (milwrite's published academic work)

**How it feels at its best:**
- Analytically precise without jargon load
- Accessible theory grounded in named specifics
- Critical but not cynical; skeptical but not dismissive
- First-person observation framed as analytical, not confessional
- Stakes are institutional and structural, but the prose names the human cost concretely

**What it is not:**
- Not corporate or policy-brief
- Not abstractly theoretical (Foucault-as-atmosphere, not Foucault-as-argument)
- Not self-congratulatory ("this paper makes a significant contribution to...")
- Not over-hedged ("it could be argued that perhaps...") — state the claim

**Emotional temperature:** Moderate. Dry wit occasionally; no performative urgency.

## Register by Project Type

### DHQ / Digital Humanities journal
- Precise, methodologically transparent
- Technical terms explained on first use; no assumed familiarity
- First-person acceptable; analytical stance throughout
- Not essayistic — closer to close reading with computational evidence

### Journalism Studies / Communication Studies
- Accessible theory in plain sentences
- Platform-studies terrain (Gillespie, Noble, Zuboff) used as framework, not cited as atmosphere
- Named specifics: subreddits, subpoenas, policies — not "social media platforms" in the abstract
- Stakes: concrete, institutional, traceable to evidence

### Essay / public-facing academic
- More personal stakes at the frame; structural analysis in the body
- Short sentences used more aggressively for rhythm
- Humor (dry, wry) used sparingly to cut through abstraction

## Tone Calibration Rules

**Use vulnerability analytically.** Personal stakes can frame insights, but the analysis carries the weight. The observation "I couldn't open my email" earns its place if it opens into a structural claim; otherwise cut it.

**Match claim scope to evidence strength.** Bounded claims in DHQ/academic ("the compliance record reveals..."); broader cultural argument in essays only when the evidence chain is explicit.

**Trust the argument.** Don't announce the argument before making it. Don't summarize the argument after making it. The prose makes the argument.

**No melodrama.** "Restructured the conditions under which X could occur" is melodrama. "Changed how X worked" states it. No sweeping systemic claims from single events.

## Humanizing Rules (make academic prose feel written by a person)

1. Let one sentence be short. After a long, clause-heavy sentence, drop a short one.
2. Name the thing specifically. "The r/ImmigrantSupport thread where students coordinate mutual aid" beats "a Reddit thread."
3. Vary sentence openers. Time marker, verb, prepositional phrase, name — not always "The."
4. Show the tension without announcing it. Don't say "this raises complex questions."
5. Use the passive selectively for institutional agents.
6. Trust transitions. Cut "In this section, I will argue that..."
7. One surprising word per paragraph — precise vocabulary that the reader didn't expect.
8. Read it aloud. If you'd pause for breath mid-sentence, break it. If it sounds like a policy brief, loosen one construction.
