# Paragraphs — Paragraph-Level Rules

*Load this for: organizing argument within a paragraph, transitions, evidence integration*

---

## Paragraph Architecture

**Concrete before abstract.** Give the example or event first, then draw the inference. Don't open with the theoretical claim and then descend to evidence.

**One idea per paragraph.** If a paragraph contains two separable claims, split it. Each paragraph should be able to answer: what is the single thing this paragraph shows or does?

**Trust transitions.** Academic writing over-explains its moves. Cut "In this section, I will argue that..." and start arguing. Don't summarize what you're about to say.

**Show the tension without announcing it.** Don't say "this raises complex questions." Ask the question, or state the two things that pull in different directions, and let the reader feel it.

## Evidence Integration

**Embed data inside the prose clause, not in parentheses or tables.**  
Data is a subordinate clause ("which receives a mean average of..."), not an interruption.

**The full pattern:**  
Place first → data nested in the location clause → mechanism as acting subject → consequence named in the subject's own terms.

"Across r/CUNY and r/Baruch, where posts average [X] per day, students' trajectories are sorted by the ranking criteria that decide which contributions reach the surface before they can gather the replies that would let a question about work, study, housing, or campus life become a usable public exchange."

What this kills: data as afterthought, consequence as abstraction, stakes framed by the analyst instead of the subject.

**Quotes work the same way.** Don't drop a block quote and then explain it from scratch. Build into the quote, then carry the consequence forward.

## Citation Rules

- No blog posts or aggregator sites doing load-bearing theoretical work
- Peer-reviewed sources for key claims
- Track edition, URL, access date
- Name the mechanism with its own language: use the platform's own UI labels when they exist ("hottest," "controversial")
- When the platform has a name for it, use that name; when the field has a term for it, use that term; when neither exists, describe it with a verb

## Counterargument

Include at least one serious rival reading per major claim cluster. Don't strawman. Name the actual objection.

**No false dilemmas in contrastive citation.**  
Two studies are competing answers only when they ask the same question with comparable methods.  
A study of eye-movement regression predictors and a study of offline behavioral responses are not in the same contest.  
**Fix:** Present each study for what it shows, in its own domain. Name precisely what any genuine tension is.

## Paragraph Endings

Don't summarize what just happened. End by extending the idea, raising the next question, or naming what the evidence commits you to.

**Bad:** "These findings suggest that platform infrastructure shapes student participation."  
**Better:** Name what the evidence specifically commits you to, then let the next paragraph open with the first concrete example of that commitment in action.
