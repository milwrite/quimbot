# Structure — Piece-Level Organization

*Load this for: structuring or evaluating a full piece — intro, arc, ending*

---

## Academic Piece (DHQ / Journalism Studies register)

### Opening

Open with a concrete detail, scene, or recognizable problem. Stakes clear by paragraph one.  
Not background, not context-setting, not a literature review. The problem, live, right away.

**Don't open with context or history.** Don't open with "Since the advent of X, scholars have...". Open with the thing itself.

**Data-in-context opening variant:** A named specific instance with embedded quantitative grounding and mechanism acting on it. Makes the claim tangible before the argument begins.

### Arc

1. **Open with friction** — failure, doubt, contradiction, or specificity that creates a question  
2. **Document the mechanism** — name what acts, show how it works, cite the actual process (algorithm, policy, decision, infrastructure)  
3. **Surface the pattern** — connect to cultural, historical, theoretical context; what framework illuminates this?  
4. **Deliver a commitment** — what does the evidence actually compel? A specific claim, a reframe, a named limit

The arc should feel like thought in motion, not a report being filed.

### Ending

End by extending or reframing. Don't summarize.  
Bad: recap the argument in three sentences.  
Good: name what the evidence commits you to that you didn't say at the start; or open the next question your argument creates; or state the concrete implication for practice.

### The One Big Idea rule (adapted)

Each piece should be able to name one memorable claim it coins, crystallizes, or demonstrates.  
Not a theme. A claim.  
"The compliance record reveals a pattern of cooperation that predates formal subpoenas."  
"Taylor's cloze tests the same underlying inference capacity that MLM pretraining learns to simulate — but for different purposes."

If you can't state it, the piece isn't ready.

### Transitions

Trust the reader. Cut "As I argued above..." and "Building on this..." when the logic makes it obvious.  
Use explicit transitions only when the logical shift is non-obvious.

## Project-Specific Arcs

### Journalism Studies / Platform/Surveillance register
- Audience: scholars + policy-adjacent, accessible theory without jargon load
- Register: Zuboff/Crawford terrain without the terminology at volume; name mechanisms in plain language
- Claim scope: bounded to what the specific evidence demonstrates; no sweeping surveillance-capitalism thesis without a clear evidence chain
- Section structure: problem, mechanism, evidence, implication

### DHQ / Digital Humanities journal register
- Register: precise, accessible, peer-reviewed citations only
- Section structure: problem, theoretical frame, method, analysis, contribution
- Every technical term explained on first use
- Claims tied to specific textual or computational evidence

## Signature Moves (milwrite's)

*Recurring structural patterns that work in milwrite's published prose. Load these as options.*

**Mechanism-as-subject opening:** Name the infrastructure/algorithm/policy as grammatical subject of the first claim. "The ranking algorithm sorts which posts surface" rather than "Participation on Reddit is shaped by..."

**Named instance before category:** Use "r/CUNY or r/Baruch" before "campus subreddits." The specific instance carries more argumentative weight.

**Evidence as subordinate clause:** Quantitative grounding enters as a nested clause at the point where consequence becomes legible, not as a citation-drop.

**Short sentence after long:** Place a short declarative after a clause-heavy sequence. The rhythm signals thinking rather than output.
