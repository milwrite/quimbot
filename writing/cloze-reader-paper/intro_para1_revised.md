### ORIGINAL (milwrite's working version)

Introduced by Wilson Taylor in 1953 as a method for measuring reading comprehension, the
cloze procedure has persisted as one of the most consequential assessment techniques to
make the move from educational psychology into machine learning infrastructure. Taylor's
famed publication in Journalism & Mass Communication Quarterly, departed from existing
approaches to reading comprehension and assessment by proposing a methodology derived
from Gestalt psychology's concept of "closure," the human tendency to complete incomplete
familiar patterns (Taylor 1953). The procedure was simple and automatable. You delete
words from a passage at regular intervals, ask readers to fill in the blanks, and
interpret their success as a measure of their sensitivity to context, syntax, and semantic
expectation. In turn, his approach recognized that human language processing operates
holistically with readers supplying missing elements based on accumulated linguistic
knowledge and the readerly capacity to reason contextually and infer the missing word in a
blanked-out passage of chosen prose (Taylor 1953). And so fill in the blank was born.

### REVISED

Introduced by Wilson Taylor in 1953 as a method for measuring reading comprehension, the
cloze procedure ranks among the most consequential assessment techniques to migrate from
educational psychology into machine learning infrastructure. Taylor's 1953 article in
Journalism Quarterly departed from existing readability approaches by proposing a
methodology derived from Gestalt psychology's concept of "closure": the human tendency to
complete incomplete familiar patterns (Koffka 1935; Taylor 1953). The procedure was simple
and automatable — delete words from a passage at regular intervals, ask readers to fill in
the blanks, and interpret their success as a measure of sensitivity to context, syntax, and
semantic expectation. Underlying this was a claim about reading itself: that human language
processing operates holistically, with readers supplying missing elements from accumulated
linguistic knowledge, syntactic expectation, and contextual inference rather than
processing words in sequence (Taylor 1953). Fill in the blank, it turned out, was a
reasonable proxy for comprehension.
