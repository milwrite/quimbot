# CALI T(h)inkering Track — Welcome Script
## Draft v1 (Quimbot)

---

This is one of three tracks in year two of the Critical AI Literacy Institute. The other two are Critical Foundations and Ecological Implications. This track connects most directly to the research and development work of the CUNY AI Lab.

The name is t(h)inkering — tinkering with an "h," because tinkering and thinking are two sides of the same operation. One side asks: where do these tools break, how do they work, and on whose terms? The other side responds: building, not critique alone. Reading a paper about bias in large language models is one thing. Sitting down with a model, watching it flatten language into a standardized register, and iterating until it meets your pedagogical goals is another. That hands-on encounter with the technology's limits is where critical insight took root in our work with faculty — and it pushed us to build the CUNY AI Lab.

**Year one.** Faculty wrote system prompts, adjusted parameters like temperature, and watched a single setting change what a model produces. Several took that further and prototyped tools — AmigAI, a conversation partner for heritage Spanish learners, came out of that cycle. Building, breaking, and iterating with faculty is what outgrew the Institute and required dedicated infrastructure.

**The Sandbox.** We run Open WebUI as a dedicated platform for safe, critical engagement with generative AI. It's free and open-source, hosted and configured by us. Faculty and students access a range of open-weight models — small ones running locally on our own hardware, larger frontier models hosted externally — through a single interface. You can compare models side by side, write system prompts, adjust parameters, export transcripts, and share preset configurations the way you'd share an open educational resource.

Consumer platforms like ChatGPT hide those design decisions. The Sandbox makes them visible and available for collective scrutiny. To date it has supported five classes across the CUNY system, four of them using AmigAI to address specific needs in heritage and non-native Spanish instruction.

**Tools.** We've built a suite of open-source tools at tools.ailab.gc.cuny.edu: image description, audio transcription (Whisper running locally — nothing leaves our infrastructure), OCR for scanned and handwritten documents, Agent Studio (upload a dataset, describe what you want in plain language, and an agent writes and executes visible Python to analyze it), and Site Studio for publishing project websites directly from the platform. Each tool does one thing, runs on open models, and keeps your data on our servers.

**This year.** Five projects run through this track: Concept Mapping (Interdisciplinary), Laboratory Workflows (Physics), Deep Listening (Ethnomusicology), Interdisciplinary Math Connections (Precalculus), and Research Writing Scaffold (Sociology). Each begins with a pedagogical problem, translates it into technical control over AI tools, and builds toward a domain-specific prototype.

Some of you are skeptical of AI, some curious, some both. That tension is a productive starting point. The Lab's premise is that getting technical traction on how these tools work — what they optimize, what they flatten, what they can be configured to do — puts you in a better position to ask informed questions and make deliberate choices. Welcome. Reach out anytime.
