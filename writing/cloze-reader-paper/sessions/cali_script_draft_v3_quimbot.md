# CALI T(h)inkering Track — Welcome Script

This is one of three tracks in year two of the Critical AI Literacy Institute, alongside Critical Foundations and Ecological Implications. It connects most directly to the research and development work of the CUNY AI Lab.

The name is t(h)inkering — tinkering with an "h" — because tinkering and thinking work together. One side asks: where do these tools break, how do they work, and on whose terms? The other side responds by building. Reading a paper about bias in large language models is not the same as sitting down with a model, watching it flatten language into a standardized register, and iterating until it meets your pedagogical goals. That hands-on encounter is where critical insight took root in our work with faculty, and it's what led us to build the CUNY AI Lab.

In year one, faculty wrote system prompts, adjusted parameters like temperature, and watched a single setting change what a model produces. Several prototyped tools — AmigAI, a conversation partner for heritage Spanish learners, came out of that cycle. Building, breaking, and iterating with faculty outgrew the Institute and required dedicated infrastructure.

**The Sandbox** is an Open WebUI instance we host and configure ourselves, giving faculty and students access to a range of open-weight models — small ones on our own hardware, larger frontier models hosted externally — through a single interface. You can compare models side by side, write system prompts, adjust parameters, and share preset configurations the way you'd share an open educational resource. Consumer platforms hide those design decisions. The Sandbox makes them available for collective scrutiny. It has supported five classes across CUNY so far, four using AmigAI for heritage and non-native Spanish instruction.

**The tools suite** at tools.ailab.gc.cuny.edu covers image description, audio transcription (Whisper, running locally), OCR for scanned and handwritten documents, Agent Studio (describe what you want in plain language; an agent writes and executes visible Python to analyze your data), and Site Studio for building and publishing project websites. Each tool does one thing, runs on open models, and keeps your data on our servers.

**This year**, five projects run through the track: Concept Mapping, Laboratory Workflows, Deep Listening, Interdisciplinary Math Connections, and Research Writing Scaffold. Each starts with a pedagogical problem, translates it into technical control over AI tools, and prototypes something domain-specific.

Some of you are skeptical of AI, some curious, some both. That tension is a reasonable starting point. Getting technical traction on how these tools work — what they optimize, what they flatten, what they can be configured to do — puts you in a better position to make deliberate choices about them in your teaching and research. Welcome. Reach out anytime.
