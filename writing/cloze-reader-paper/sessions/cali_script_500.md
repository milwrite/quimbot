# CALI T(h)inkering Track — 500-word version

This is one of three tracks in year two of the Critical AI Literacy Institute, alongside Critical Foundations and Ecological Implications. It connects most directly to the research and development work of the CUNY AI Lab.

The name is t(h)inkering — tinkering with an "h" — because tinkering and thinking work together. One side asks: where do these tools break, how do they work, and on whose terms? The other side responds by building. Reading a paper about bias in large language models is not the same as sitting down with a model, watching it flatten language into a standardized register, and iterating until it meets your pedagogical goals. That hands-on encounter is where critical insight took root in our work with faculty, and it's what led us to build the CUNY AI Lab.

In year one, faculty wrote system prompts, adjusted parameters like temperature, and watched a single setting change what a model produces. Several took that further. AmigAI, a conversation partner for heritage Spanish learners, came out of that cycle of building, breaking, and iterating in close collaboration with instructors. That process outgrew the Institute and required dedicated infrastructure.

**The Sandbox** is an Open WebUI instance we host and configure ourselves, giving faculty and students access to a range of open-weight models — small ones running on our own hardware, larger frontier models hosted externally — through a single interface. You can compare models side by side, write system prompts, adjust parameters, export transcripts, and share preset configurations the way you'd share an open educational resource.

Consumer platforms like ChatGPT hide those design decisions. The Sandbox makes them visible and available for collective scrutiny. To date it has supported five classes across CUNY, four using AmigAI for heritage and non-native Spanish instruction. We work directly with instructors to surface the technology's tendencies — the way it flattens dialect-specific Spanish, for instance — and to identify where guardrails are needed.

**The tools suite** at tools.ailab.gc.cuny.edu covers image description (a vision-language model generates alt text or visual summaries), audio transcription (Whisper running locally, so nothing leaves our infrastructure), OCR for scanned and handwritten documents, Agent Studio (describe what you want in plain language; an agent writes and executes visible Python to analyze your data), and Site Studio for building and publishing project websites. Each tool does one thing, runs on open models, and keeps your data on our servers.

**This year**, five projects run through the track: Concept Mapping (Interdisciplinary), Laboratory Workflows (Physics), Deep Listening (Ethnomusicology), Interdisciplinary Math Connections (Precalculus), and Research Writing Scaffold (Sociology). Each starts with a pedagogical problem, translates it into technical control over AI tools, and builds toward a domain-specific prototype.

Some of you are skeptical of AI, some curious, some both. That tension is a reasonable starting point. Getting technical traction on how these tools work — what they optimize, what they flatten, what they can be configured to do — puts you in a better position to make deliberate choices in your teaching and research.
