# CALI T(h)inkering Track — 500-word version

The third track is T(h)inkering — tinkering with an "h" — a frame for treating building and thinking as the same act. One side asks: where do these tools break, how do they work, and on whose terms? The other responds by making: translating a scholarly or pedagogical question into something you can test, configure, and iterate. The t(h)inkering track invites you to consider what a discipline-specific AI tool could do for your teaching, and what building one might teach you about the technology itself.

The clearest illustration of that is AmigAI. Instructors teaching heritage and non-native Spanish classes came to us with a problem: consumer AI tools flatten dialect-specific Spanish. Out of the box, a model corrects toward a standardized register and away from what their students actually speak and write. Working directly with those instructors, we built a conversation partner that holds space for the Spanish their students bring into the classroom. That collaboration required surfacing exactly what the technology was doing — how it encodes a standardized norm, where it resists deviation, what it takes to retrain its defaults toward a pedagogical goal. AmigAI is now in active use across several CUNY courses, and it remains a running collaboration between the Lab and the instructors who use it.

That project also pushed us to build dedicated infrastructure. The CUNY AI Lab is a standalone extension of the Institute, providing safe and secure shared access to large language models and the tools built on top of them.

The Sandbox gives faculty and students access to a range of open-weight models through a single interface. You can compare models side by side, write system prompts, adjust parameters, and share preset configurations like open educational resources. Consumer platforms hide those design decisions. The Sandbox makes them visible for collective scrutiny.

The tools suite at tools.ailab.gc.cuny.edu covers audio transcription (Whisper, running locally), image description, OCR for scanned and handwritten documents, Agent Studio (describe what you want; an agent writes and executes visible Python to analyze your data), and Site Studio for publishing project websites. Each tool does one thing and keeps your data on our servers.

Together these resources support what we call critical making: using AI as a coding scaffold, a research tool, and an object of inquiry at the same time.
