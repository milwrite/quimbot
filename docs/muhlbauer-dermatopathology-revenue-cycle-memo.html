Consulting memo

Dr. Jan Muhlbauer Lab billing operations memo

Assessment of current claim-routing operations, denial handling, patient-responsibility leakage, and the operational implications of building a more structured billing workflow.

Prepared for
Internal leadership review
Prepared by
Quimbot
Basis
Interview transcript + external RCM research
Scope
Billing routing, denials, patient AR, collections

Executive summary

Dr. Jan Muhlbauer Lab’s billing pressure appears to come from four interacting causes: high rejection volume, heavy manual triage after rejection, weak recovery on patient-responsibility balances, and concentration of workflow knowledge in one experienced supervisor. The process is functioning, but it is functioning in a way that is difficult to scale, difficult to transfer, and vulnerable to loss when expertise is unavailable.

Assessment

The central issue is not simply collections. The central issue is the absence of a documented, standardized decision system for routing rejected and unpaid claims through the correct next action quickly enough to preserve recoverable revenue.

What we learned from the transcript

The interview reveals a practical three-branch routing model already in use:

  • rebill to another insurance company
  • move the balance to patient responsibility
  • send the balance toward collections after patient billing ages out

Jan also describes a recurring fallback sequence: insurance first, patient ledger for roughly two to three months second, collections third. That sequence matters because it defines the real operational spine of the billing system.

“Those are the main categories that things go to.”

The transcript also makes clear that claim triage is knowledge-intensive. Rejections often arrive with weak explanation. Insurance data is frequently wrong or stale. High-deductible plans produce large patient balances that often do not convert to cash. Exceptional large balances are sometimes negotiated downward informally.

Primary business problems

1. Knowledge concentration

One supervisor appears to hold the operational logic for routing rejected claims. Others help execute, but she decides what happens. This creates key-person risk and weakens continuity.

2. Manual post-rejection workflow

Rejected claims appear to return to staff in a way that still requires manual review and interpretation. This creates avoidable delay and labor cost.

3. Front-end data errors create back-end rework

Wrong insurance, switched insurance, inactive insurance, and missing insurance create downstream denials that consume staff time later. These are often preventable errors.

4. High-deductible patient balances behave like structural leakage

Many balances transferred to patients under deductible logic appear to recover poorly. That means a meaningful share of the ledger is aging into low-probability cash.

5. Exception handling is informal

Large patient bills may be reduced or partially written off after a complaint or physician intervention. That suggests the lab is operating without a formal hardship, negotiation, or escalation policy.

6. Reimbursement pressure may shape case-level behavior

The transcript suggests that some payer classes reimburse so poorly that they influence how much additional testing the lab is willing to order. That creates operational and governance risk.

Why this matters

The current model leaves revenue dependent on staff memory, timing discipline, and discretionary intervention. That is a poor foundation for reliability.

How comparable labs typically handle this

External pathology and laboratory revenue-cycle guidance points in a consistent direction, even when the sources are vendor-facing rather than academic.

Front-end prevention

Labs with stronger revenue-cycle performance emphasize real-time eligibility checks, benefits verification, prior-authorization rules where relevant, and diagnosis / coding checks before claim submission.

Formal denial categories

Better-performing shops separate eligibility failures, coding issues, authorization denials, patient responsibility, duplicate claims, medical-necessity issues, and excessive-unit problems rather than treating all rejections alike.

Rules-based routing

Other labs increasingly use claim scrubbing, payer-specific edits, and reason-code mapping so each denial type goes to the right owner with the right next step.

Templated appeals and analytics

Pathology RCM vendors describe templated appeal logic, attached documentation bundles, dashboards, and reporting by payer, denial type, and success rate.

Implication for Dr. Jan Muhlbauer Lab

The lab does not need to become fully automated all at once. It needs to formalize decision categories, owners, thresholds, and evidence rules first. That alone would reduce fragility and improve consistency.

Known unknowns

  • What exact criteria move a claim from rejected insurance status to rebilling another insurer?
  • What exact criteria move a claim from insurance work to patient ledger?
  • What are the top rejection reasons by count, by dollars, and by preventability?
  • What data survives after resolution, and can it be queried historically?
  • What deadlines govern refiling, appeal windows, patient aging, and collections handoff?
  • What policy governs large-balance reduction, hardship handling, or partial write-offs?
  • Which payer classes or plan types are structurally low-yield or loss-making?

Unknown unknowns

The broader scenario implies deeper risks that may not appear in current reporting.

Shadow policies

The lab may already be following unwritten rules that staff know but nobody has documented, such as when to stop pursuing insurance, when to infer Medicare, or when to settle a large patient balance quickly.

Hidden variation in decision-making

Different staff may resolve similar cases differently. That would produce inconsistent recovery and weak accountability, even if the overall process appears stable.

Invisible underpayment leakage

The visible denial pile may not capture underpaid claims, claims never pursued, or claims written down quietly because they are too painful to work. If so, the lab’s true leakage exceeds what staff currently see.

Clinical-business spillover

If reimbursement logic is shaping how much workup the lab is willing to perform for certain payer classes, the issue is larger than billing efficiency. It becomes an operations-governance problem.

Most important hidden risk

The lab may effectively be running three billing systems at once: the formal system in software, the practical system in staff memory, and the exception system triggered by physician or patient complaints.

Recommended action plan

  1. Document the routing logic with the billing lead immediately. Capture each branch, trigger, and fallback rule in plain language.
  2. Create a formal rejection taxonomy. Do not treat all rejections as a single work queue.
  3. Separate patient-responsibility balances from true denials. These are different operating problems and should be tracked separately.
  4. Define timing thresholds and owners. Every rejection category should have a deadline, an owner, and a standard next action.
  5. Create a high-balance exception policy. Standardize when to escalate, reduce, settle, or write off.
  6. Assess the data exhaust. Determine whether historical resolved rejections can be analyzed. If not, begin structured logging now.
  7. Build tooling only after the policy model exists. The right first tool is likely a routing assistant or dashboard, not a large automation build.

Conclusion

Dr. Jan Muhlbauer Lab does not appear to have a broken billing operation. It appears to have a functioning but underformalized one. The core opportunity is to convert experienced human judgment into a documented operating model with explicit branches, thresholds, and ownership. Once that is done, the lab can decide which parts should remain supervisory and which parts can be standardized or automated.

Sources used: internal transcript provided in thread; external pathology and lab RCM guidance including XiFin, ADSC, and 3Gen materials on denial handling, claim scrubbing, coding accuracy, and appeals operations.
