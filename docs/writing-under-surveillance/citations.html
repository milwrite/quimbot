"AI Detection Tools." USD Law Library Guides (second citation — "cheeky" claim) lawlibguides.sandiego.edu/… Quimbot
"Better yet, randomly inserting the word "cheeky" fooled detectors 80–90% of the time ("AI Detection Tools," USD Law Library)."

The 80–90% figure and the "cheeky" word are confirmed present in the USD Law Library guide, sourced there from Cat Casey (chief growth officer at Reveal, New York State Bar AI Task Force), originally reported by Isha Marathe in Law.com, Nov. 10, 2023. The figure and word are real.

Paraphrase materially misrepresents the source. The article says Casey used "cheeky" by "randomly inserting" it. The source says she added "cheeky" to her prompt — meaning she instructed the AI to generate text using the word, not that she inserted it into already-generated or human-written text. This is a meaningful distinction: one describes manipulation of AI output at the generation stage; the other implies post-hoc insertion into any text.

The original Law.com source: Marathe, Law.com, Nov. 10, 2023.

Recommendation: Revise to: "…by adding the single word 'cheeky' to a generation prompt, which fooled detectors 80–90% of the time" or similar.

Weber-Wulff, Debora, et al. "Testing of Detection Tools for AI-Generated Text." International Journal for Educational Integrity, vol. 19, no. 1, 2023. eprints.whiterose.ac.uk/… · arxiv.org/abs/2306.15666 Quimbot
"…tested fourteen publicly available tools alongside Turnitin and PlagiarismCheck, if only to conclude that the tools are "neither accurate nor reliable," exhibiting a systematic bias toward classifying AI-generated text as human-written rather than detecting it (Weber-Wulff et al.)."

Both URLs confirmed accessible. White Rose repository (University of Leeds) and arXiv preprint both live. DOI: 10.1007/s40979-023-00146-z. Published: 1 December 2023 (online: 25 December 2023).

Direct quote verified. arXiv abstract states verbatim: "The researchers conclude that the available detection tools are neither accurate nor reliable and have a main bias towards classifying the output as human-written rather than detecting AI-generated text." This matches the article's quotation exactly.

Tool count note: The article says "fourteen publicly available tools alongside Turnitin and PlagiarismCheck." The arXiv abstract says "12 publicly available tools and two commercial systems (Turnitin and PlagiarismCheck)" — 12 + 2 = 14 total. The article's phrasing slightly implies 14 + Turnitin + PlagiarismCheck (i.e., 16), but reading in context ("fourteen publicly available tools alongside…") it appears to mean 14 tools total, which includes Turnitin and PlagiarismCheck as part of the 14. This is ambiguous but not inaccurate. Note: the deep research source cites it as "14 publicly available tools and two commercial systems" which is incorrect per the arxiv abstract; the article's phrasing is closer to correct.
