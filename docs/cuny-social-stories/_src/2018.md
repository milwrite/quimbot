In 2018 the recurring motif becomes openly procedural. Students are no longer only asking whether others feel the same way; they are building small operational manuals for surviving CUNY's offices and billing systems. The year sits before the pandemic but already shows a university whose ordinary administrative channels feel difficult to reach.

A student on the main CUNY subreddit asks about a bill sent after enrolling and quickly dropping a semester. The student says the charge is large enough to matter and that the college has warned about collections. The question is practical and fearful: does CUNY actually send these debts out, and what can be done if no credits were taken? A later commenter urges sustained calling rather than relying on email, explains that a collections placement may still be correctable through the college, and notes the broader risk of credit damage. The advice is bureaucratic self-defense.

At Baruch, another user posts a campus office workaround. They describe a loop of calling a general number, leaving messages, missing return calls, and beginning again. Their solution is to search directories and call individual employees one by one until someone answers. A reply adds that in-person visits during quieter hours may work better because offices cannot as easily disappear behind voicemail.

Late in the fall, Baruch students perform a similar translation around ePermit. A user has approval but still cannot enroll at the host campus and does not know whether the problem is timing, term selection, or the permit itself. A reply points to the enrollment-date page, the term-change control, and a host-college date that looked alarming until a phone call clarified that the schedule was not final. The answer is not simply "call someone"; it shows how to read a confusing official screen without mistaking every strange date for a closed door. This extends 2017's Brooklyn DegreeWorks refresh trick: small portal details become peer-taught survival knowledge.

The story in 2018 is not that peer advice replaces official authority; it is that students must learn unofficial routes to reach that authority at all. The bill-collections thread, Baruch phone-directory workaround, and ePermit clarification all turn frustration into collective technique. They also show students teaching one another how to distinguish a final decision from a portal artifact. That habit will expand in 2019, when the Queens College WiFi petition and the CUNY financial-aid hold thread move from individual fixes to infrastructure pressure and account-status interpretation.
