The 2024 corpus is dense with high-engagement CUNY-wide debate: campus politics, vandalism, professor conduct, salary transparency, underfunding, and whether college still pays off. Within that noise, the strongest motif remains student-made scaffolding around damaged or insufficient institutional support.

One main CUNY post describes a transfer student returning to Lehman after injury, community college repair, and a much stronger later GPA. The student follows an advisor's guidance about dropping and swapping classes after a department chair offers a prerequisite waiver. The plan fails when late registration blocks the student because the system still reads an older low GPA. The student loses full-time status and is left with a casual acknowledgment that the advice was wrong. The comments translate the story into a broader rule: students have learned not to rely entirely on advisors. One user says their real advisor is the degree audit tool; another describes spending time on the subreddit untangling errors made by offices that should have prevented them.

At Baruch, an alum offers free resume and career help to CUNY students and graduates. The post lists resume review, networking, raises, internships, salaries, public speaking, and financial planning as topics for group sessions. The offer is not only generosity; it is a student-built supplement to career infrastructure, especially for commuters and first-generation professionals who may not inherit these scripts elsewhere.

By June, the main CUNY subreddit also hosts an alumni salary thread. The prompt asks graduates to name school, major, graduation year, job title, and current pay. The scale of response turns curiosity into a crowd-built labor-market dataset. One commenter calls it unusually useful because the information is both local and comparable: CUNY students can see not an abstract career outcome but the messy spread of what alumni report after specific degrees. This extends the Baruch resume-help offer from an advising session into salary transparency, and it answers the same need as 2023's finance-career guide: students want a way to price their path without relying only on brochures or prestige narratives.

In 2024, the scaffold has three sides: defensive auditing against bad institutional advice, alumni-led care work for career entry, and peer-produced salary evidence. It looks back to 2023's professor-accountability debate, ransomware speculation, and finance translation while making the cost of official misguidance painfully concrete. The forward line to 2025 is already visible: when students share browser tools for Schedule Builder, monitor federal aid panic, and circulate textbook-cost workarounds, they keep building instruments around systems they cannot fully trust.
