After the first emergency year, 2021 turns help into routine practice. Students and alumni share money, professor memory, and portal instructions while the community works through remote fatigue, vaccination rules, federal relief grants, and the uneven return to campus.

In January, a Baruch alum posts an offer to cover textbook or transportation costs for students facing financial difficulty. The offer is striking because it bypasses official aid bureaucracy. It assumes that small costs can decide whether a semester is manageable, and it asks students to reach out privately rather than perform need publicly. Comments thank the alum and recognize the gesture as a use of personal stability to reduce someone else's immediate strain. One commenter admits that book costs on top of tuition are already pushing them toward distress.

In May, another Baruch graduate leaves behind a personal professor archive. The list is not an official evaluation system; it is a transfer student's memory of courses, instructors, assignments, attendance rules, speech requirements, grading habits, and which classes felt useful or forgettable. A commenter celebrates the post precisely because it converts one student's completed path into a map for people still registering. The gesture extends 2020's low-cost textbook thread and the Queens College CSC 370 self-teaching discussion because when official course descriptions do not say enough, students annotate the curriculum with lived experience.

By November, the main CUNY subreddit turns to federal emergency grant money. A student posts instructions for locating an expected ARPA grant in CUNYfirst, translating a buried portal pathway into a peer-readable route. The comments then become a miniature financial aid desk where users compare amounts, clarify whether funds will go to bank accounts or tuition balances, and warn others to read the relevant form carefully. This is institutional information, but it circulates through student narration before it becomes usable.

The year reframes mutual aid as money, interpretation, and memory. One user offers to pay for books or transit; another turns professor experience into a registration resource; another teaches people how to see money already assigned to them. The old commuter problem of hidden know-how becomes a shared archive. After 2020's Hunter homelessness post, August textbook-source collection, and February CSC 370 self-teaching thread, 2021 shows a community learning to routinize care while the emergency has not really ended. In 2022, that routine will be tested by the Hunter unsafe-home crisis, the redesigned CUNYfirst interface, and the September financial-aid refund manual.
