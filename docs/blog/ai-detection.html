Writing Under Surveillance

The Problem with AI Detection

"There has always been resistance to teaching machines and to the technocracy in which they are embedded… And perhaps it's worth repeating that that resistance did not come only from disgruntled educators."

— Audrey Watters, Teaching Machines

Whether on Reddit or by word of mouth, CUNY learners describe anxiety and frustration around the prospect of being falsely accused by AI detection software, their professors entrusting algorithmic systems over the word and testimony of their students.

Plagiarism detection software like Turnitin have long shaped how students learn to write and under what conditions they see themselves as writers. More recently, the plot has thickened, and these platforms have begun marketing AI detection features that claim to distinguish human-written text from machine-generated output. Now, international students report accusations of academic dishonesty for syntax that is "too clean," while others testify to misspelling words or introducing mistakes in their writing to evade false positives, even when every word is their own.

When students feel the need to perform their humanity for an audience of algorithms, something has gone awry, as students are writing not for their human peers, but for automated systems, seldom even understood by professors who mandate them or administrators who procure them. The harms those systems inflict — from wrongful suspicion to self-censorship — often fall hardest on working-class and non-native English-speaking students like so many enrolled across the CUNY system.

To understand the problem with AI detection, consider what these services claim to measure. For example, GPTZero claims to use two metrics: one is perplexity, a measure of how well a language model would predict each successive word in a passage; another is burstiness, which tracks creative variability in sentence rhythm and structure (Tian). This logic relies on the assumption that human writers intuitively exhibit syntactic variance and mix long and short sentences together, while AI models tend to propagate consistent, flat tempos at the sentence- and paragraph-level ("AI Detectors"; Galczynski).

This premise is incredibly shaky, and also constitutive of AI detection failures at scale. For instance, one of the most comprehensive accounts in the field tested twelve publicly available tools alongside Turnitin and PlagiarismCheck, reporting they were "neither accurate nor reliable" and exhibited a systematic bias toward classifying AI-generated text as human-written (Weber-Wulff et al.). It's worth noting here that even a "low" 1% false positive rate across 22.35 million first-year college essays amounts to 223,500 essays falsely flagged in a single year (Hirsch).

Those affected are not just edge cases at the margins of detection either. The MLA-CCCC Joint Task Force on Writing and AI put it plainly when it said AI detection tools enable "false accusations" that "may disproportionately affect marginalized groups," with Black students facing disproportionately higher rates of AI-detection accusations than their white peers (Madden et al.). At the same time, neurodivergent students have been reported as more likely to receive a false positive for submitted writing that departs from the narrowed templates these tools inscribe as human (Hirsch). Likewise, a Stanford study of seven widely used detectors found they classified 61.22% of TOEFL essays by non-native English speakers as AI-generated (Liang et al.). Across all seven detectors, 89 of 91 essays were flagged by at least one of the tools sampled in the study.

This matters because AI detection relies on a construct of authenticity narrowed to features the algorithm can count, weight, and score, and such legibility is not evenly distributed, especially among student groups as diverse as those at CUNY. The "human" quality these tools claim to detect is less a quality inherent in student writing than a predefined likeness the system measures their prose against, as though it were always already there. Students are left to manage the burden of a system that casts doubt on styles and registers of prose through which they might otherwise express themselves.

Why, then, do institutions keep insisting that detectors work as intended? Part of the answer lies in the pedagogical logic these tools presume and produce. AI detection treats writing as an object of surveillance, invites suspicion in place of trust, and frames assessment as a matter of automated verification rather than pedagogical judgment.

Is this really the future we want for our students as learners, or for ourselves as educators?

No matter their alleged sophistication, AI detectors ultimately seek to classify and identify patterns in language that are neither stable across large language models (LLMs) nor robust to the quick tricks or jailbreaking methods of those bent on gaming the system (Weber-Wulff et al.). They are anxiety-inducing, mercurial, and bear reified standards for human writing that are as opaque as they are normativizing. And as language models grow more capable of simulating human prose, the distinction between machine- and human-generated text becomes only more difficult to maintain. At that point, the question then arises whether AI detection poses a solvable problem at all. It very likely does not.

Building trust in the classroom is hard work, and AI detection services undermine that effort at every turn, much like a wolf in sheep's clothing, a problem dressed in its own solution. We and our students are better served when we take time to slow down, start small, and hold space in that pause for reading and writing as social projects still in the making. All the while, it'd do us well to leave the problem of AI detection at the door.
