# Structure

*How pieces move. Read this before drafting.*

---

## Foundational Principles

- **Concrete before abstract** — give the event, example, or mechanism first; inference follows.
- **Stakes by paragraph one** — don't clear your throat. The reader should know why this matters within the first 150 words.
- **End with forward momentum** — not summary; extension or reframe.
- **Move from example to idea**, not idea to example.

---

## Academic Paper Arc

Standard structure for DH/DHQ register:

1. **Open with friction or a specific claim** — a moment of failure, surprise, or a narrow concrete question. Not "In this paper, I will argue..." Just argue.
2. **Establish the mechanism** — name the specific thing being analyzed (the algorithm, the procedure, the dataset). Not the category.
3. **Connect to context** — now zoom out to field, history, or theory. Only after the reader has seen the thing.
4. **Make the move** — the claim, clearly stated. Bounded to the evidence.
5. **Counterargument slot** — at least one serious rival reading per major claim cluster.
6. **Conclusion: what follows** — not summary. What does this imply? What would disprove it? What's the next question?

### What kills academic openings
- Starting with a definition ("The cloze procedure, first developed by...")
- Starting with a context survey ("Scholars have long noted...")
- Starting with the paper's organizational summary ("This paper is structured as follows...")

## Microblog Arc

1. **Iframe at top** (when covering a gallery artifact — required)
2. **Open with the specific object** — what is this thing? One sentence.
3. **Why it caught attention** — the observation, the surprise, the question it raises.
4. **Research / historical context** — one or two paragraphs; source the relevant work; no lists.
5. **The core idea** — the one concept the piece crystallizes.
6. **Code snippet** — pull the actual implementation, not pseudocode. See `paragraphs.md` for code-in-prose rules.
7. **Close with the artifact link** — not summary; forward gesture.

## Structural Templates (named patterns)

### The Discovery Arc
Use for: describing a process of learning or building something new.

1. Problem or frustration encountered (or bad habit confessed)
2. Conventional solution inadequate (or current framing misleading)
3. Breakthrough moment
4. New practice developed, named
5. Results and what changed (with specifics)
6. How the reader can adopt it

### The Technical Deep-Dive
Use for: explaining how something works, step by step.

1. What the reader needs to care about before they understand the mechanism
2. The mechanism, named and described with its own terminology
3. The edge cases that reveal what's really going on
4. Implications — what changes now that you know this

### The Two-Position Frame
Use for: placing work in relation to existing literature.

1. What's established in the field (bounded claim)
2. What this work shows that the field hasn't addressed (not "fills a gap" — state the specific move)
3. The relationship between them (complementary? corrective? extending?)

## Openings to Avoid

- Definition openers ("X is a method for...")
- Survey openers ("Many scholars have argued...")
- Organizational roadmap openers ("This paper proceeds as follows...")
- Rhetorical question openers as stalling move ("What does it mean to read in the age of AI?")

---

## Endings to Avoid

- Restatement of thesis
- "In conclusion"
- "Further research is needed"
- Tone of satisfied completion — end with energy, not a bow
