# CL-04 — Counterargument slot

**Category:** Claim  
**Added:** 2026-04-06  
**Non-negotiable:** No (revision habit — required for academic profiles)

---

## Rule

Every major claim cluster in an academic argument requires at least one serious rival reading. The counterargument must be the strongest available version of the opposing position, not a straw-man. Acknowledge it; address it; move on.

---

## Diagnostic

Identify the paper's three or four major claims. For each: what is the most credible objection? Is that objection present in the text? Is it addressed with more than a dismissive gesture?

---

## What "serious rival reading" means

Not: "Some might argue that X" (vague, no attribution)  
Yes: "Agrawal et al. (2019) argue that deletion method plays no role in cloze difficulty above a certain window size — a finding that would challenge the claim that every nth deletion systematically targets different processes"

The rival has:
- A named source or a specific version of the argument
- A reason it has force
- An engagement that explains why the paper's claim still holds

---

## Examples

**Weak counterargument slot:**
> Some may argue that the cloze task is too simple a measure to ground claims about reading comprehension. However, as this paper shows, it remains useful.

**Strong counterargument slot:**
> Perfetti et al. (1979) argued that cloze completion reflects lexical access rather than comprehension, which would limit the task's interpretive range. Taylor's own norming data complicates this: completion rates correlate with passage difficulty as rated by independent raters, suggesting the task is responsive to text-level factors beyond individual word frequency.

---

## Placement

The counterargument slot can go anywhere in the argument where the rival reading is most naturally raised — typically after the main claim is stated, before the full evidentiary support is given. Don't bury it at the end as a disclaimer.
