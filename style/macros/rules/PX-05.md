# PX-05 — Earned punctuation / no lazy cues

**Category:** Punctuation  
**Added:** 2026-03-29 (supersedes 2026-03-18 colon/semicolon audit in scope)  
**Non-negotiable:** Yes

---

## Rule

Em dashes, semicolons, and colons are not substitutes for prose. They paper over logical relationships that should be expressed as syntax or connector words.

**Default disposition hierarchy:**

1. **Restructure** — rewrite so no special punctuation is needed
2. **Logical connector** — use "because," "so that," "since," "which reveals," "to"
3. **Leave the mark** — only when restructuring would break legitimate rhythm

---

## Diagnostic

For each em dash, semicolon, or colon in a sentence:

- What logical relationship is the mark performing?
- Can that relationship be expressed as a subordinating conjunction or prepositional phrase?
- If yes, express it. If no, the mark is earned.

---

## Em dash (PX-01)

**Earned:** Parenthetical aside where removal is lossless; strong rhetorical break in a short sentence.  
**Unearned:** Default replacement for any other punctuation; whenever a comma or period would work; whenever the clause could be restructured.

**Bad:** The report was finished — finally — and submitted.  
**Good:** The report was finished and submitted, three weeks late.

---

## Colon (PX-02)

**Earned:** Introducing a list; colon + restatement where the colon compresses what preceded it.  
**Unearned:** Papering over a logical connector.

**Bad:** simple and automatable: delete words at regular intervals  
**Good:** simple and automatable, requiring only that words be deleted at regular intervals

---

## Semicolon (PX-03)

**Earned:** Two short parallel clauses with no expressible logical connector; rhetorical punch in a two-part sentence.  
**Unearned:** Joining clauses that have a real cause/consequence/contrast relationship.

**Earned example:** "The player is not simulating the inference engine; they are one."  
**Unearned:** rewrite with "because," "since," "so that," or split into two sentences.

---

## Single separator rule (PX-04)

At most one structural separator (colon, semicolon, enclosing em-dash pair) per clause. Two separators in one clause means two logical relationships have been papered over. Split and surface both.

**Bad:** She finished the report — which took three weeks — and, crucially, submitted it on time: ahead of the deadline.  
**Good:** She finished the report in three weeks and submitted it ahead of the deadline.
