# VB-02 — Strong verbs

**Category:** Verb  
**Added:** 2026-04-06  
**Non-negotiable:** No (revision habit — enforced via VB-01 audit)

---

## Rule

Choose verbs that carry the full weight of the action. Short, physical, concrete verbs are stronger than abstract, process-oriented ones. The verb should be the most descriptively precise option, not the most polite or general one.

---

## Preferred verb inventory

**For argument and analysis:**
show, find, trace, identify, test, measure, compare, reveal, distinguish, contradict, confirm, challenge, complicate, dispute, narrow, extend, break, cut

**For building and making:**
build, write, run, ship, fix, test, push, pull, drop, grab, set, open, close, boot, kill, load, parse, emit

**For movement and change:**
move, shift, fall, rise, drop, climb, spread, narrow, widen, split, merge, compress, expand

**For data and evidence:**
record, track, log, score, rank, filter, sort, flag, match, count, aggregate, sample, skew

---

## Examples of upgrades

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| The model performs well on X | The model scores [X%] on |
| The platform allows users to | Users can / The platform lets users |
| The paper seeks to examine | The paper examines |
| The study attempts to show | The study shows (or: the authors tested whether) |
| Changes were made to | X changed Y |
| A decision was reached | The committee decided |

---

## Edge cases

Hedged language in academic prose ("may suggest," "appears to indicate") is not automatically weak. Epistemic hedges earn their place when the claim genuinely warrants uncertainty. The rule targets verbs where the hedge is evasion rather than precision. "The data suggest" = acceptable hedge; "The results seem to perhaps indicate" = verb cowardice.
