# CT-01 — Submarine rule

**Category:** Citation  
**Added:** 2026-03-21  
**Non-negotiable:** Yes (all academic profiles)

---

## Rule

Don't pit studies against each other that answer different questions. A submarine doesn't outperform Michael Phelps at swimming — it does something else entirely.

Two studies are in genuine tension only when they:
- Answer the same research question
- Use comparable dependent variables
- Draw from comparable populations and measures

If any of these differ, describe each in its own domain.

---

## Examples

**Bad:**
> Veldre et al.'s finding of alignment contradicts Jacobs et al.'s divergence result.

These study different things. No contradiction exists.

**Good:**
> Jacobs et al. examined behavioral alignment between LLM predictions and human cloze responses. Veldre et al. examined whether LLM-derived predictors fit eye-movement data better than human norms. The studies address different phenomena.

## The operational test

See CT-02 for the same-question pre-writing procedure. CT-01 states the principle; CT-02 makes it actionable.

---

## Edge case: genuine tension

When studies do conflict — same question, same DV, similar populations — name the tension precisely:

> Both studies measured [X] in [population]. Jacobs et al. found [result A]; Veldre et al. found [result B]. The discrepancy likely traces to [specific methodological difference].

Never leave the conflict at "contradicts." Name the mechanism.
