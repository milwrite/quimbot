# SX-04 — Nominalized subject

**Category:** Syntax  
**Added:** 2026-03-20  
**Non-negotiable:** Yes (all profiles)

---

## Rule

Do not open a sentence with a nominalization — a verb or adjective turned into a noun phrase — as the grammatical subject. Find the agent or action and lead with that.

---

## Diagnostic

Does the subject end in -tion, -ment, -ness, -ity, -al, -ance, -ence, or -ing (gerund)? If so, ask: what is actually doing the thing? Name that instead.

---

## Examples

**Bad:**
> The optimization of the model proceeds in two stages.

> The application of these rules to spoken language is contested.

> The reliance on behavioral data alone creates interpretive gaps.

**Good:**
> The model optimizes in two stages.

> Linguists contest how these rules apply to spoken language.

> Relying on behavioral data alone creates interpretive gaps. [gerund as subject is acceptable when there is no better agent]

---

## Fixes

1. Identify the verb buried in the nominalization ("optimization" → "optimize")
2. Find the agent performing that action
3. Rewrite: agent + active verb + object

---

## Exception

Technical terms of art where the nominalization is the standard name in the field ("nominalization" itself, "fixation," "regression," "comprehension") are acceptable. The test: does the field's published literature use this noun form as its primary term? If yes, use it.

---

## Edge case: gerund subjects

"Relying on behavioral data alone creates gaps" is acceptable because "relying" names the action directly without burying it in an abstract suffix. The ban targets nouns derived from verbs/adjectives via suffixation, not gerunds used as compressed infinitives.
