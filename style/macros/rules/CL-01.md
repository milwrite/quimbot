# CL-01 — Earned assertion gate

**Category:** Claim  
**Added:** 2026-03-29  
**Non-negotiable:** Yes (all profiles)

---

## Rule

Before a sentence asserts a conclusion, the surrounding prose must have supplied the reader with the evidence or observation that earns it. An assertion that arrives before its grounds is vacuous, regardless of whether the claim is true.

---

## Diagnostic

Read the sentence in isolation. If it states a conclusion:

1. Look back. Has preceding prose *shown* the thing being concluded, or only *named* it?
2. If only named — the evidence sentences are missing. Write them first.

---

## Examples

**Bad:**
> The difference is concrete. A language model updates weights during pretraining; a reader recovers a word choice.

The assertion ("the difference is concrete") arrives before the evidence.

**Good:**
> A language model updates weights during pretraining. A reader recovers a word choice. These are not two performances of the same operation at different skill levels — they are categorically different activities.

Evidence sentences come first. The conclusion earns its assertion because the reader has already seen the two operations in parallel.

---

## What this consolidates

- Vacuous-opener ban ("The difference is clear.")
- "Which is to say" overuse (paraphrasing a claim instead of earning it)
- Submarine rule (CT-01): false dilemmas between studies that haven't been set up

All share the same failure: assertion without earned grounds.

---

## Non-negotiables

- **Never assert and then evidence.** Always evidence then assert.
- **No one-sentence paragraphs that state a conclusion** without a preceding evidence chain (unless the conclusion is a compression of a long prior argument the reader just read).
