# DC-12 — No bare demonstrative backward pointers

**Category:** Diction  
**Added:** 2026-04-06 (from STYLE_GUIDE.md 2026-03-31)  
**Non-negotiable:** No (revision habit — extends DC-05)

---

## Rule

Using "that" or "this" as the sole anchor pointing back to something named in the preceding sentence is banned. Replace the demonstrative + noun with a noun phrase that names the referent explicitly.

---

## Pattern to kill

Subject: "That [noun]..." or "This [noun]..." where the demonstrative is doing all the referential work.

**Banned examples:**
- "That gradient shows..."
- "That claim holds..."
- "That division stages..."
- "That shared premise required..."
- "This opacity is itself part of..."
- "This delegation is this paper's primary subject."

---

## Examples

**Bad:**
> The live app delegates word selection to a language model. This delegation is this paper's primary subject.

> Pretraining absorbs texts into parameter weights before any reader encounters them. That prior absorption is the point.

**Good:**
> The live app delegates word selection to a language model — and that design choice, not the app itself, is this paper's primary subject.

> Pretraining absorbs texts into parameter weights before any reader encounters them. The prior absorption of these texts into parameter weights is the point.

---

## Test

Remove the sentence and look at what it's pointing to. Can you name the thing without the demonstrative? If yes, write that name directly.

---

## Note on legitimate uses

"This" and "that" as modifier adjectives mid-sentence ("in this framework," "at this scale," "in that period") are not the same problem — the rule targets sentence-subject and sentence-opening backward pointers where the demonstrative is the only identification.

---

## Related rules

- DC-05 ("This" requires a noun): the parent rule. DC-12 extends it to "that" and to cases where the noun is present but still too vague.
- PR-06 (no freestanding echo sentences): bare demonstrative sentences often echo the preceding sentence rather than advancing the argument.
