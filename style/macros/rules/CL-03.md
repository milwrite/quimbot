# CL-03 — No floating assertions

**Category:** Claim  
**Added:** 2026-04-06  
**Non-negotiable:** No (revision habit)

---

## Rule

Every non-trivial claim requires traceable evidence — a named source, a specific dataset, an observation the reader can verify. A claim without a traceable source is a floating assertion and must be either grounded or cut.

---

## Diagnostic

Underline every sentence that makes a factual claim. For each underlined sentence: can you name what the evidence is and where to find it? If no, the sentence is floating.

---

## Examples

**Floating:**
> Platform governance has historically favored compliance over user protection.

> Predictive models consistently outperform human annotators on this task.

> Participation rates drop significantly in highly moderated communities.

**Grounded:**
> Reddit's transparency reports (2012–2022) show compliance with government requests rising from 88% to 96%.

> Veldre et al. (2022) found that LLM-enabled surprise measures explained eye-movement variance better than cloze norming in three reading corpora.

> Wu et al. (2019) found that participation rates dropped by 31% in subreddits after strict enforcement policies were introduced.

---

## What counts as traceable

- Named authors + publication/preprint
- Named dataset with access information
- FOIA documents with release dates
- Your own collected data with collection method stated

**Does not count:**
- Blog posts as load-bearing evidence for empirical claims
- Wikipedia
- Unnamed "studies show"
- Aggregator sites summarizing primary research

---

## Floating assertion vs. accepted knowledge

"The earth is approximately 4.5 billion years old" does not need a citation. The test: is this claim contested in the relevant field? Is it empirical and non-obvious? If yes, it needs a source.
